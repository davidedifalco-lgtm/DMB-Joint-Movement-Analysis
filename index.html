Correlation Analysis — Verdict

Are they just generally failing, or does movement correlate?

The answer is both, and the nuance matters:

1. General failure: YES. 88% of all RC joints show cracking — across every zone. This supports mageba's argument that the RC material is fundamentally unsuitable for this dynamic environment. The 2020 PU pilot joints (#17, #26) in the SAME zones show zero cracking, proving the loading environment is survivable with the right material.

2. Severity correlates with movement: YES. The worst damage (score 3) clusters in the West Truss Span (joints 11, 14, 15, 18) and at suspended span cable transitions (PP7, PP15, PP41-49). These are zones with higher vibration PPV and/or higher differential movement between panels.

3. The suspended span pattern is the most revealing. Damage is NOT uniform — it peaks near tower/cable transitions and diminishes at mid-span. This matches the structural mechanics: maximum differential vertical movement between adjacent panels occurs where cable stiffness transitions create abrupt changes in deflection profile.

What this means strategically:
— The RC material fails everywhere (general unsuitability), but it fails faster and worse where movements are highest (correlation).
— The PU pilot joints surviving in the same zones is your strongest evidence that this is a material problem, not a "bridge moves too much" problem.
— However, challenge this: the PU pilots are only 3 years old (installed 2020). The RC joints are newer (Phase 1, installed during construction). If the PU pilots were performing well at age 3 but the RC joints failed within days/weeks, the comparison still strongly favors PU — but long-term PU durability under these conditions remains unproven.
